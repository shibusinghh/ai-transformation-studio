# LinkedIn Draft — Cisco and the sequencing problem

**Status:** draft · **Target publish:** 2026-07-08 · **Source lab note:** 2026-07-07-cisco-agent-rollout.md

---

Cisco begins layoffs on July 13. Two weeks later, its new AI agent reaches every remaining employee.

Nobody at Cisco needs a memo to connect those two dates. Proximity writes the story for them: "the agent is why your colleagues left."

Here's what that does to the rollout, from a change management seat:

1. Every adoption metric is now contaminated. Low usage reads as resistance. High usage reads as fear. Neither tells you if the tool works.

2. The usual messengers are compromised. You cannot ask the same managers who delivered termination news to say "trust the tool" in the same month.

3. Agents only create value when people expose their real workflows to them. A scared workforce feeds the agent sanitized, minimal work — and the ROI case quietly dies.

The uncomfortable truth: most AI rollout failures are sequencing failures, decided months earlier in a budget meeting where no one asked the adoption question.

If the dates can't move, three levers remain: name the awkward timing yourself before the rumor mill does, shrink the mandatory rollout to an opt-in cohort whose results you publish, and sign — publicly — what agent usage data will never be used for.

If leadership won't sign that last one, that's not a communications problem. That's the finding.

I spent six years doing change management at Deloitte and EY. The agent era doesn't retire that discipline — it raises the stakes on it.

What would you do if the CFO wouldn't move the date?
